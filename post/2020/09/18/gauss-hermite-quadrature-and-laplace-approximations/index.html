Gauss-Hermite Quadrature and Laplace Approximations

2020/09/18

There always seems to be a better vantage point–even well-established photo spots seem to be missing something (perhaps just because they are so widely accepted!). Still, that’s the challenge at hand–to pick your spot.

Anyways, today I feel like the best viewpoint for understanding the uses of Gauss-Hermite quadrature in statistics comes from a 1994 paper, “A Note on Gauss-Hermite Quadrature,” by Qing Liu and Donald A. Pierce.

Assume we wish to approximate the following integral:

\[\int_{-\infty}^\infty g(t)dt\] In its most basic form, Gauss-Hermite quadrature is a way to approximate integrals of the form

\[\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\exp(-x^2)dx\]

essentially by using a weighted sum

\[\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\exp(-x^2)dx\approx \sum_{i=1}^m w_if(x_i)\]

where \(x_i\) are commonly referred to as nodes (chosen here to be zeroes of the mth order Hermite polynomial) and \(w_i\) represent corresponding weights.
