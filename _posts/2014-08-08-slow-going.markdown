For the past eight weeks, I've been working as an intern at the [Data Science for Social Good Summer Fellowship](www.dssg.io). Over the course of a summer, fellows at DSSG--generally recent graduates and graduate students--hone their data science skills, employing statistics and computer science in order to examine a variety of real-world problems through a quantitative lens. As an intern, however, my responsibilities are largely administrative: think ordering lunch and booking flights.  

Still, these jobs rarely occupy my entire day; instead, I find myself in possession of large chunks of free time, time with which I've begun attempting to absorb what I can about data science. For the most part, this has meant toying around with the tools used by fellows here at DSSG and trying to teach myself how to program. The latter undertaking has proven mystifying at times, but most of my difficulties have not involved actually writing code. Rather, my struggles revolve around more superficial tasks like installing programs and understanding how to integrate them.

An extremely abbreviated list of some of my actual questions from the past months:

<div style="margin-left: 2em;">
<ul>
<li>What is a terminal?</li>
<li>Why doesn't Windows have one?</li>
<li>How do I ssh?</li>
<li>When should I use Git?</li>
<li>Where can I find the answers to these questions?</li>
</ul> 
</div>

Stupid questions? Possibly. It's extremely likely that many of these problems could easily have been solved by using Google more efficiently, but I can't help but feel like there's a deficiency when it comes to resources for individuals in situations like mine. While there may be an abundance of options for learning programming languages like Python (try [Codecademy](www.codecademy.com) or [MIT OpenCourseWare](www.ocw.mit.edu)), I found it far more challenging to find easily accessible answers to the sorts of questions I had. 

So, progress has been slow, probably in large part due to my own obliviousness. I have nevertheless truly enjoyed this entire experience--the seminars and talks I've sat in on DSSG have generally been compelling (for info on some of the speakers I found most interesting, see [here](http://www.papachristos.org/Research.html) and [here](http://www.johnmyleswhite.com/notebook/2012/07/28/my-new-book-developing-deploying-and-debugging-multi-armed-bandit-algorithms/). The mentors and fellows here at DSSG have been excellent teachers, and I've had the chance to begin working on some small side projects. At present, I've begun exploring Chicago City Council data with one of DSSG's mentors and my fellow intern. A preliminary thought: City Council procedures are strangely obscure and esoteric, which has complicated any data analysis we've tried to perform. If this project pans out, I'll write up any interesting findings (I know you're on the edge of your seat now).

At this point, my data science know-how amounts to writing crude snippets of code and having a vague understanding of various techniques used by the fellows here. An extremely basic level of knowledge, to be sure, but all the same, I'm excited about all that I don't know. With so much to learn, gradual progress looks to be the theme of the day. No complaints here.
